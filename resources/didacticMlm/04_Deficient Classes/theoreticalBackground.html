2. Theoretical Background

The term ‘object’ serves as a linguistic bridge between an everyday notion of object and a technical notion of object. This may sometimes lead to a confusion of software and physical objects in that classes may reproduce deficiencies of natural-language categories. A too narrow replication of everday objects as software objects can lead to redundancies and, as a result, poses a threat to the system’s integrity. Classes that reproduce deficiencies of natural-language terms, and with that lead to conceptual redudancy, are called deficient classes. Cosider as an example the natural-language term “book,” which may refer to a particular book copy I can hold in my hand or also to the intellectual artifact that a copy represents. As such, both of the following expressions are valid in everyday communication: “She is reading a book.” (referring to a particular book copy) “Moby Dick is a book.” (not referring to any particular printed copy, but to the intellectual artifact) To replicate this ambiguity in object models may lead to redundancies and thus threatens the integrity of the system. Example: A library manages books that can be borrowed. A single class Book may serve to represent all exemplars the library possesses and include the attributes “title: String” and “id: String”. The attribute “title: String” would, however, lead to redundancies and threaten system integrity since multiple copies (each with its own id) share the same title (because they are copies of the same intellectual artifact). The example model shows how an improved version of such a scenario might look like.
